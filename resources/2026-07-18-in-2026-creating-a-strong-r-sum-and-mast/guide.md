# Proof Over Paper: A 2026 Job-Search Playbook

The post's core argument: résumés are becoming the least trustworthy signal you send. AI writes them, AI filters them, and many managers auto-dismiss anything that smells bot-generated. The winning move is sending proof a machine can't fabricate — work samples, portfolios, and answers that survive follow-up questions.

This guide turns that thesis into an actionable system.

## 1. Understand what actually happens to your résumé

- **ATS parsing first.** Most mid-to-large employers screen with an Applicant Tracking System before a human reads anything. Poor formatting (tables, columns, images, headers/footers) can make you invisible.
- **Bias risk is real.** The iTutorGroup EEOC settlement ($365,000) is a documented case of automated age discrimination. Don't hand systems unnecessary signals (e.g., graduation dates from decades ago) unless required.
- **AI-detection skepticism.** A large share of hiring managers distrust obviously AI-written copy. Use AI to draft, then rewrite in your own voice with specifics only you know.

## 2. Build a résumé that survives the machine (but doesn't rely on it)

Checklist:

- [ ] Single-column, standard section headings (Experience, Education, Skills).
- [ ] Plain text job titles and company names — no graphics carrying key info.
- [ ] Quantified outcomes: "cut deploy time 40%" beats "responsible for deployments."
- [ ] Mirror the exact keywords from the job description (skills, tools, certifications).
- [ ] No fabrication — every claim must survive a follow-up question in an interview.
- [ ] Export as a text-selectable PDF; test by copy-pasting into a plain text file.

## 3. The proof layer (the part the post says actually matters)

Attach or link evidence that can't be auto-generated:

| Signal | Example | Why it beats a résumé |
|--------|---------|----------------------|
| Work sample | A real bug fix, a written analysis, a design | Demonstrates judgment, not claims |
| Portfolio | GitHub, case studies, published writing | Verifiable and dated |
| Take-home artifact | A small project done well | Shows how you work, not just outcomes |
| Referral/testimonial | A named person vouching | Machines can't fabricate reputation |

**Rule of thumb:** every headline claim on your résumé should map to one piece of external proof.

## 4. Interviews: answers that survive follow-up questions

The test isn't a polished story — it's whether your story holds up when someone digs. Use the STAR-D method:

- **S**ituation — context
- **T**ask — your specific responsibility
- **A**ction — what *you* did (not "we")
- **R**esult — quantified outcome
- **D**epth — be ready for: *Why that approach? What failed? What would you change?*

If you can't answer the depth follow-ups, the story isn't yours to tell — pick a different one.

## 5. A weekly job-search cadence

1. Maintain a living portfolio (update after every meaningful project).
2. Tailor résumé keywords per role — 10 minutes, not a rewrite.
3. For each application, attach or link one proof artifact.
4. Prep 5 STAR-D stories covering leadership, failure, conflict, technical depth, impact.
5. Track responses; iterate on what earns callbacks.
