# Worked Scenario: The Dual-Effect Consent Form

A practical exercise for ethicists, product teams, and clinical writers grappling with an intervention that cures *and* enhances.

---

## The scenario

A gene therapy upregulates a protein (call it Factor-K) to halt early-stage neurodegeneration. In trials it also, reliably:
- extends healthy lifespan by a measurable margin, and
- improves memory and processing speed in *unaffected* cognitive domains.

The patient in front of you has early Alzheimer's markers. They qualify for treatment. What does the consent form say?

---

## Draft A — Therapy-only framing (inadequate)

> "This therapy is intended to slow the progression of neurodegenerative disease. Risks include [X, Y, Z]."

**Problem:** Omits known, characterized effects. If the longevity and cognitive gains are reliable and documented, hiding them is a disclosure failure — the patient cannot weigh what they're actually accepting.

---

## Draft B — Full disclosure framing (better)

> "This therapy is intended to slow neurodegenerative disease. Based on trial data, recipients may also experience: (1) extended healthy lifespan, (2) improvements in memory and cognitive speed beyond restoration of baseline. These effects are not the goal of treatment, cannot currently be separated from the therapeutic effect, and are not reversible independently of the therapy. Consider whether you accept these outcomes."

**Why better:** Names the enhancement, admits inseparability, admits irreversibility, and hands the value judgment to the patient.

---

## The five questions applied

| Question | Answer for Factor-K |
|---|---|
| Is enhancement foreseeable? | Yes — reliable in trials. Must be disclosed. |
| Reversible / opt-out? | No — bundled with therapy. Disclose bundling. |
| Disclosure complete? | Only in Draft B. |
| Distributive fairness? | Access gated by diagnosis → the healthy can't get lifespan gains, the diagnosed get them "free." Flag for policy. |
| Baseline drift? | If widely adopted, enhanced cognition may redefine "normal." Long-horizon concern. |

---

## The uncomfortable conclusion

Once an enhancement effect is *reliable and characterized*, the ethically clean move is not to suppress it or ban the therapy — it's to **disclose it fully and let the patient decide.** The debate then shifts from "should humans enhance?" (unanswerable in the abstract) to "who gets access, on what terms, and who is disadvantaged by gatekeeping enhancement behind diagnosis?" (answerable with policy).

That is the real work the philosophical framing tends to avoid.

---

## Discussion prompts

1. Would you sign Draft B? Would you sign it for a child who cannot consent?
2. If the enhancement is desirable, is it fair that only the sick can obtain it?
3. At what point does refusing an available cognitive enhancement become a competitive disadvantage — and does that make consent meaningfully free?
