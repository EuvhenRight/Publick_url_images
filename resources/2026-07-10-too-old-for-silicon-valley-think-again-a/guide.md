# The Broken Ladder: A Practical Guide to Building Experience When AI Eats the First Rung

This guide turns the LinkedIn post's provocative question—*"if AI ate the first rung of the ladder, how does anyone climb to become the experience the market now pays for?"*—into concrete, actionable advice for junior developers, hiring managers, and teams.

---

## 1. What the data actually says (and doesn't)

The post cites several figures worth stating carefully so you don't overclaim:

- Tech's over-40 share fell from ~56% (2014) to ~52% (2022), then the average workforce age started climbing.
- Pave data (as cited in reporting): Gen Z share at large public tech firms dropped from ~15% to under 7% between 2023 and 2025.
- A Stanford analysis of ADP payroll data found roughly a 20% employment drop for developers aged 22–25, while older cohorts held steadier.

**Critical caveat:** This period overlaps a broad hiring slowdown and high interest rates. Correlation is not causation. AI is *a* plausible factor, not a proven sole cause. Treat these numbers as directional, not gospel.

---

## 2. The core dynamic: judgment is being repriced

The market increasingly pays for:

- **Judgment** — knowing which problem to solve and when a solution is "good enough."
- **Domain context** — understanding the business, the users, the failure modes.
- **Review & verification** — catching what an AI confidently gets wrong.

AI is cheap at *producing* first drafts of code. It is weak at *deciding* and *owning* outcomes. The skills that survive are the ones that compound with experience.

---

## 3. If you're early-career: compress the ladder

The old ladder (write boilerplate → get feedback → slowly build judgment) is being cut. Replace it with a faster loop:

1. **Use AI to produce, then interrogate.** For every generated block, ask: *Why this approach? What breaks it? What would a senior reject here?* Write your answer down. This is deliberate practice for judgment.
2. **Ship end-to-end, small.** Own a feature from ticket to production, including monitoring. Ownership generates judgment faster than volume of code.
3. **Specialize into context.** Pick a domain (payments, healthcare, infra) and go deep. Domain context is the moat AI doesn't have.
4. **Read production incidents.** Post-mortems are concentrated experience. Read every one you can access.
5. **Practice review.** Reviewing others' (and AI's) code trains the exact skill being repriced upward.

## 4. If you're hiring or leading a team: don't starve your own pipeline

The post's sharpest irony: *"We built the intern and then stopped hiring the intern."* If the whole industry cuts entry-level roles, in five years there are no seniors to promote.

Practical moves:

- **Redefine junior work.** Junior roles should now emphasize verification, testing, incident triage, and AI-output review—not raw boilerplate.
- **Pair juniors with AI *and* a senior.** The AI accelerates output; the senior transfers judgment.
- **Measure ownership, not lines.** Reward juniors for owning outcomes.
- **Fund an explicit apprenticeship track.** Treat pipeline as capital investment, not overhead.

---

## 5. Honest framing for your own posts

If you write about this topic, keep intellectual honesty:

- Distinguish *cited reporting* from *your interpretation*.
- Name the confound (macro hiring slowdown).
- Avoid "AI killed junior jobs" as a headline conclusion; say "the data is consistent with, but does not prove, AI pressure on entry-level roles."
