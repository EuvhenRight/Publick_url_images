# Who's Holding the Wheel? A Practical Guide to Reclaiming Attention

This guide turns the ideas in the post into concrete, repeatable practices. It has two audiences:

1. **People trying to defend their own attention** from persuasive design.
2. **Builders** who want to earn attention honestly instead of manufacturing it.

---

## Part 1: The Two Definitions of "Engagement"

The post's core insight is that one word does two jobs:

| Whose word | What it means | Who measures it | Incentive |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Growth team's "engagement" | Number of times you returned / interacted | Dashboards, retention curves | Revenue |
| Your "engagement" | Staying in one thought long enough to finish it | Nobody, unless you do | Your goals |

The asymmetry: only one side has a metric and a paycheck riding on the outcome. If you don't measure your own attention, the only scoreboard that exists is the one built to extract it.

---

## Part 2: A Field Guide to Common Attention Traps

Name the pattern to disarm it.

- **Manufactured scarcity** — "Only 3 rooms left," countdown timers. Ask: *Is the deadline real, or invented to short-circuit deliberation?*
- **Sunk-cost onboarding** — "Save and personalize" so leaving feels like abandoning a home you built. Ask: *Am I staying because it's useful, or because I've been furnished into it?*
- **Variable reward loops** — pull-to-refresh, unpredictable notifications. Ask: *Would I open this if the timing were predictable?*
- **Infinite feeds** — no natural stopping cue. Ask: *What was I actually here to do?*
- **Social obligation nudges** — "3 people are waiting for your reply." Ask: *Whose priority is this?*

## Part 3: A Self-Audit

Run this weekly. Honest answers beat perfect ones.

1. What did I finish this week that required sustained focus?
2. Which apps/interfaces decided where my effort went before I did?
3. Where did I mistake *frequent* return for *valuable* return?
4. What one interruption default can I turn off this week?
5. What am I building that would earn attention even with notifications disabled?

## Part 4: For Builders — Earning Attention Honestly

The Nieman Lab argument in the post: chasing platform attention is an unreliable bet; durable value comes from doing good work. Practical translation:

- **Design a natural stopping point.** A finished state is a feature, not lost engagement.
- **Make the exit as clean as the entrance.** Easy cancellation and export build the trust that earns real return.
- **Prefer real scarcity to invented urgency.** If it isn't true, don't say it.
- **Measure completion, not just time-on-site.** "Did the user accomplish their goal and leave satisfied?" is a healthier north star.
- **Send fewer, higher-signal notifications.** Every ping is a withdrawal from a trust account.

See `honest-metrics.md` for a starter scorecard.

---

## Part 5: The One Question

> The interface will always want you back. What are you building that earns your attention more honestly than it does?

Keep it somewhere you'll see it. It reframes every design and every day from *how do I capture attention* to *how do I deserve it.*
